The Research Question

Does Sanofi's Turing AI recommendation engine — which delivers Next Best Action (NBA) and Next Best Channel (NBC) suggestions to field sales representatives — cause an increase in patient initiations for Wayrilz (rilzabrutinib)?

We cannot run a randomized controlled trial. Turing was rolled out based on business rules, not random assignment. So we use Propensity Score Matching (PSM) — a quasi-experimental method that constructs a comparable control group from observational data.

PSM — Propensity Score Matching. A statistical technique that pairs treated units (HCPs who received Turing suggestions) with control units (HCPs who did not) based on their estimated probability of receiving treatment, given observed characteristics.
ATT — Average Treatment Effect on the Treated. The causal quantity we estimate: among HCPs who received suggestions, how much did the suggestion change their initiation rate? Reported in percentage points (pp).

2
Core Assumptions of PSM

PSM produces valid causal estimates only if four assumptions hold. Violating any one of them undermines the entire analysis. These are not optional — they are the logical foundation that separates PSM from a naive comparison.

1. Unconfoundedness (Ignorability)
Unconfoundedness — Also called the ignorability or conditional independence assumption. It states: conditional on observed covariates X, treatment assignment is independent of potential outcomes. In plain language: after we account for the 11 covariates in our model, there are no remaining hidden factors that simultaneously drive both (a) whether an HCP receives a Turing suggestion and (b) whether that HCP's patients initiate on Wayrilz. If an unmeasured confounder exists — say, a formulary change at certain hospitals — PSM cannot remove that bias.
This is untestable. We can never prove that all confounders are observed. The E-value (Section 9) quantifies how strong an unmeasured confounder would need to be to overturn our result — but it cannot prove one does not exist. This is why we describe results as ILLUSTRATIVE.
2. Positivity (Common Support / Overlap)
Positivity — Every HCP must have a non-zero probability of being in either group. Formally: 0 < P(Treatment=1 | X) < 1 for all values of X in the sample. If some HCPs have a propensity score of exactly 0 or 1, they cannot be matched — there is no comparable unit in the other group. This is especially critical for IPW and AIPW, where extreme scores (near 0 or 1) produce enormous weights that destabilize the estimate.
Common Support — The region of propensity score values where both treated and control HCPs exist. Matching only occurs within common support. HCPs outside this region are dropped. In our analysis, the caliper (0.2 × SD of propensity scores) enforces common support by refusing to match treated HCPs with no sufficiently close control.
3. SUTVA (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption)
SUTVA — Two components: (1) No interference — one HCP's treatment does not affect another HCP's outcome. (2) No hidden treatment variations — the"treatment" (receiving a Turing suggestion) means the same thing for every HCP. SUTVA is potentially violated when multiple HCPs share the same Healthcare Account (HCA), because one rep's behavior toward one HCP at an account may influence their behavior toward other HCPs at the same account.
Known risk: Our current unit of analysis is the individual HCP. An HCA-level analysis (pending Ashutosh's linking table) would address the interference concern by analyzing at the account level where the rep's decision is made.
4. No Model Misspecification
The propensity score model must be correctly specified — it must include all relevant confounders and model their relationship to treatment assignment adequately. A misspecified PS model produces biased scores, which means matches are not truly comparable. This is why we sweep 22 models across two families (logistic + GBM): if multiple specifications yield similar ATT estimates, the result is more credible regardless of which model is"correct."
Why These Assumptions Matter

Selection bias is the core problem PSM addresses. Because Turing suggestions were not randomly assigned — they followed business rules based on rep workload, HCP segment, geography, and other factors — the treated and control groups differ systematically. A naive comparison of initiation rates between groups would confuse the effect of treatment with the effect of these pre-existing differences. PSM attempts to remove selection bias by making the groups comparable on observed covariates.

Confounding occurs when a variable influences both treatment assignment and the outcome. For example, HCPs with higher call volume (a confounder) are more likely to receive suggestions AND more likely to have initiations simply because they interact more with reps. If we do not adjust for call volume, we would falsely attribute the higher initiation rate to Turing rather than to the underlying engagement pattern. Our 11 covariates are chosen specifically to capture the major confounders.

Endogeneity arises when the treatment variable is correlated with the error term — meaning there are unobserved factors affecting both treatment and outcome. In our context, endogeneity would occur if we defined treatment as"rep actioned the suggestion" rather than"HCP received a suggestion," because actioning is a post-treatment behavior influenced by unobserved rep motivation. The ITT (Intention-to-Treat) design avoids this specific form of endogeneity.

Study Design & Treatment Definition
Treatment: ITT (Intention-to-Treat)
ITT — Treatment is defined as receiving ≥1 Turing suggestion after Nov 1, 2025, regardless of whether the rep accepted, dismissed, or ignored it. This avoids endogeneity — we do not condition on the rep's post-treatment response.
Why not define treatment by"actioning"? Proactive reps who accept suggestions may also be more proactive in driving initiations. Conditioning on a post-treatment behavior makes the estimated effect uninterpretable.

Outcome: Binary — did the HCP have ≥1 patient initiation in the post-period (Nov 2025 – Mar 2026)?

Key constraint: Rare disease initiations have a ~2-month lag. With only ~5 months of data, we may lack the power to detect a real effect. Results are ILLUSTRATIVE — more data and ELAAD integration will strengthen the test.

Covariate — A pre-treatment variable that might predict both treatment assignment and the outcome. We include covariates so matched pairs are similar on these characteristics, isolating the treatment effect.
SMD — Standardized Mean Difference. Formula: (mean_treated − mean_control) / pooled_SD, where pooled_SD = √((SD²_treated + SD²_control) / 2) — the root-mean-square of both groups' standard deviations. Propensity Score Models (22 Swept)
Two Model Families
Logistic Regression — Linear classifier. One hyperparameter: C (inverse regularization strength). Smaller C = stronger penalty = simpler model. Swept: C = 0.01, 0.1, 1.0, 10.0 → 4 models.

Logistic regression fits a sigmoid (S-shaped) curve to binary treatment labels. For each HCP, the model takes 11 covariate values, computes a weighted sum, and passes it through the sigmoid function to produce a propensity score between 0 and 1. The C parameter controls how flexible the curve is.

GBM (Gradient Boosting Machine) — Ensemble of sequential decision trees. Three hyperparameters: n_estimators (number of trees; 1st number), max_depth (tree depth; 2nd number), learning_rate (shrinkage; 3rd number). Notation: GBM (50/2/0.1) = 50 trees, depth 2, rate 0.1. Grid: 3×3×2 = 18 models.

GBM builds trees sequentially — each new tree is trained on the residuals (errors) from the previous ensemble. The learning rate (η) shrinks each tree's contribution to prevent overfitting. After 50 trees of depth 2, the ensemble's combined prediction is passed through a sigmoid to produce a propensity score. The selected model uses GBM (50/2/0.1) = 50 trees, depth 2, learning rate 0.1.

Total: 4 + 18 = 22 PS models. All fitted on 4,829 HCPs with 11 covariates predicting treatment status.
AUC — Diagnostic, NOT Selection Criterion
ROC Curve — Receiver Operating Characteristic. A plot of the true positive rate (sensitivity) versus the false positive rate (1 − specificity) at every possible classification threshold. It shows how well the model distinguishes treated from control HCPs across all decision boundaries.
AUC — Area Under the ROC Curve. Summarizes the ROC curve as a single number between 0 and 1. AUC = 0.5 means the model is no better than random guessing. AUC = 1.0 means perfect discrimination. For PSM, moderate AUC (0.6–0.8) is actually ideal — very high AUC means the treated and control groups are so different that matching will struggle to find comparable pairs. AUC is reported for information — it never drives model selection.
5-Fold Cross-Validated AUC — Split data into 5 folds; each takes a turn as test set. Average of 5 test AUCs gives an honest estimate. In-sample AUC inflates GBM performance. We always report CV AUC as primary.

Logistic Regression vs. GBM — Head to Head

We swept both model families to let the data decide which produces better balance and more matched pairs. Here is a structured comparison of the two approaches for propensity score estimation, followed by why GBM won in this analysis.

Why GBM won in this analysis: GBM (50/2/0.1) produced the most matched pairs (1,524) with all covariates passing balance (max|SMD| = 0.065). The best logistic model (C=10) matched 1,507 pairs — comparable, but its maximum SMD was slightly higher. The key advantage is GBM's ability to model the non-linear relationship between segment, call volume, and treatment probability without manual interaction terms. However, the close performance of both families is itself a robustness finding — if both model types produce similar estimates, the result is less sensitive to model specification.
Regularization: The Same Idea, Two Implementations

Regularization is any technique that constrains a model to prevent it from fitting noise in the training data (overfitting). Both logistic regression and GBM use regularization, but implement it differently.

In logistic regression, regularization is an L2 penalty (also called ridge penalty) that adds a cost proportional to the square of the coefficients. The hyperparameter C is the inverse of the penalty strength: C=0.01 means heavy penalty (simpler model), C=10 means light penalty (more flexible). The effect is to shrink all coefficients toward zero, preventing any single covariate from dominating.

In GBM, regularization comes from three mechanisms working together: learning_rate (η, also called shrinkage) scales each tree's contribution down — at η=0.1, each tree only contributes 10% of its full prediction, requiring many trees to build up the final answer. max_depth limits how complex each individual tree can be — depth 2 means each tree can make at most 2 sequential splits, capturing only simple interactions. n_estimators controls how many trees are built — stopping early prevents the ensemble from memorizing training patterns. Together, these three knobs control the bias-variance tradeoff.

Estimation Methods — Matching vs. Weighting vs. Stratification

Once we have propensity scores, there are three fundamentally different strategies for using them to estimate causal effects. Each has distinct mechanics, assumptions, and tradeoffs. We use all three as a robustness check — if they agree, the conclusion is not an artifact of any single method.

Detailed Method Definitions

Nearest Neighbor 1:1 Matching

NN 1:1 (without replacement) — For each treated HCP, find the single control HCP with the closest propensity score."Without replacement" means each control can only be used once — once matched, it is removed from the pool. A caliper of 0.2 × SD(PS) prevents poor matches: if no control falls within this distance, the treated HCP goes unmatched. The ATT is simply the mean difference in outcomes between treated and their matched controls: ATT = (1/N) Σ(Y_treated_i − Y_control_i).
Caliper — A maximum distance threshold for matching. Set to 0.2 × standard deviation of propensity scores, a rule-of-thumb from Rosenbaum and Rubin (1985) that removes approximately 98% of bias from a normally distributed confounder. Too tight a caliper → fewer matches (less power). Too loose → poor-quality matches (more bias). The 0.2×SD default is the widely accepted balance point.
NN 1:1 (with replacement) — Same algorithm, but each control can be matched to multiple treated HCPs. This reduces bias (the best match is always available) but inflates precision — in our data, only 293 unique controls were used for 2,639 treated HCPs, meaning some controls were reused 9+ times. This violates the independence assumption for standard error estimation and is why we flag this estimator rather than relying on it.

Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW)

IPW — Inverse Probability Weighting. Instead of matching, IPW keeps every HCP and assigns weights. For estimating the ATT, treated HCPs get weight = 1 (they are the reference population), and control HCPs get weight = PS / (1 − PS), where PS is their propensity score. Controls that look most like the treated group (high PS) get the highest weight. The weighted outcome mean of controls is then compared to the treated mean. In our data, the maximum weight was 5.11 and mean was 1.13 — no extreme weights, indicating good overlap.
When IPW breaks: If any control HCP has a propensity score very close to 1, the weight PS/(1−PS) explodes. For example, PS=0.99 produces a weight of 99. A single HCP with an extreme weight can dominate the entire estimate. Weight truncation (capping weights at a threshold) is one mitigation, but it introduces bias. Our maximum weight of 5.11 indicates this is not a concern in our data.

Augmented IPW (AIPW) — Primary Estimator

AIPW — Augmented Inverse Probability Weighting. Combines IPW with an outcome model (a separate logistic regression that predicts the outcome Y given covariates X). The"augmentation" adds a bias-correction term: if the PS model is slightly wrong, the outcome model compensates, and vice versa. This is the doubly robust property — the estimate is consistent (converges to truth) if either the PS model or the outcome model is correctly specified. You do not need both to be right, just one.
Outcome Model — A separate logistic regression (distinct from the PS model) that predicts the probability of the outcome (initiation) given the 11 covariates. In AIPW, this model is used to construct predicted outcomes under treatment and control for each HCP, which serve as the"augmentation" that corrects for any residual imbalance the PS weights did not fully address.
Doubly Robust — A statistical property meaning: the estimator gives the correct answer if at least one of two component models is correctly specified. For AIPW, the two models are: (1) the propensity score model (probability of treatment), and (2) the outcome model (probability of initiation). In practice, neither model is perfect — but doubly robust estimators provide a"safety net" by allowing one model to compensate for the other's errors. This is why AIPW is our primary estimator.

Stratification

Stratification (5 strata, Cochran default) — Proposed by Cochran (1968), who showed that dividing a continuous variable into 5 equal-frequency bins removes approximately 90% of bias. The PS range is split into 5 bins; within each bin, treated and control HCPs have similar propensity scores. The ATT within each bin is the simple mean difference, and the overall ATT is the weighted average across bins, with weights proportional to the number of treated HCPs in each bin: ATT = Σ(n_k/N_treated) × ATT_k.
Fine Stratification (10 strata) — Same approach but with 10 bins instead of 5. More strata reduces within-stratum bias (HCPs within each bin are more similar) but risks sparse strata (some bins may have very few treated or control HCPs, producing noisy estimates). This is a standard bias-variance tradeoff. With 4,829 HCPs and 10 bins, we average ~483 per bin — sufficient to avoid sparsity.
Full Matching (not used in this analysis) — An algorithm that creates matched sets with variable ratios (1:many or many:1), minimizing total PS distance across the sample. Unlike stratification, which uses fixed bin widths, full matching optimizes the grouping. We do not implement full matching; our"Fine Stratification (10 strata)" is sometimes confused with it but uses a simpler equal-width binning approach.

Standard Errors and Confidence Intervals

Standard Error (SE) — A measure of the precision of our estimate. It quantifies how much the estimated ATT would vary if we drew different samples from the same population. Smaller SE = more precise estimate. SE is used to construct confidence intervals: CI = ATT ± 1.96 × SE for a 95% interval under normal approximation.
Bootstrap (1,000 resamples) — A resampling technique: draw 1,000 random samples with replacement from the original data, each the same size as the original (4,829 HCPs)."With replacement" means the same HCP can appear multiple times in one sample and be absent from another. For each resample, re-estimate the ATT. The standard deviation of these 1,000 ATT estimates is the SE. Bootstrap CIs in our analysis use the normal approximation: ATT ± 1.96 × bootstrap SE, rather than percentile-based CIs (which would use the 2.5th and 97.5th percentile of the 1,000 estimates directly). The bootstrap is used for NN matching because analytical SEs for matched pairs are debated in the literature.
Analytical SE — For IPW and AIPW, standard errors are computed from closed-form formulas (the influence function / sandwich estimator) rather than resampling. These are faster, do not depend on a random seed, and are well-established for these estimators. For stratification, the SE uses the delta method — a technique for approximating the variance of a function of random variables.
Confidence Interval (CI) — A range [lower, upper] that would contain the true ATT in 95% of repeated experiments. When our 95% CI includes zero (as all of ours do), we cannot reject the null hypothesis that Turing has no effect. A CI is NOT the probability that the true effect is inside the interval — it is a statement about the long-run frequency of correct coverage.

Sensitivity Analysis
Unmeasured Confounding & the E-value
Risk Ratio (RR) — The ratio of the outcome probability in one group to another. If treated HCPs have a 5% initiation rate and controls have 3%, the risk ratio is 5/3 = 1.67. A risk ratio of 1 means no difference. Risk ratios are used in the E-value framework because they have a natural scale for quantifying confounder strength.
E-value — Developed by VanderWeele and Ding (2017), the E-value answers: "How strong would an unmeasured confounder need to be to explain away the observed treatment effect?" Formally, it is the minimum risk ratio that an unmeasured confounder would need to have with both the treatment and the outcome — simultaneously — to fully account for the ATT. The formula is: E-value = RR + √(RR × (RR − 1)), where RR is the observed risk ratio of the treatment effect. Our E-value of 3.66 means a hidden confounder would need to at least triple the odds of both treatment and outcome. Since our ATT is near zero, the E-value is more of a methodological completeness check than a practical concern — the real question is whether missing covariates could reveal a true effect, which is why ELAAD integration matters.
SUTVA — Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption. (1) No interference between units — one HCP's treatment cannot affect another HCP's outcome. (2) No hidden treatment variations — the"treatment" (receiving a Turing suggestion) is the same for every HCP. SUTVA is at risk when multiple HCPs share HCAs (Healthcare Accounts), because a rep who changes behavior toward one HCP at an account may change behavior toward all HCPs at that account.
SUTVA violation biases toward zero. If control HCPs at the same account as treated HCPs are indirectly"treated" by rep spillover, we are comparing two partially-treated groups — diluting the true effect. HCA-level analysis (pending Ashutosh's linking table) would address this by defining treatment at the account level.
Refutation Tests — Stress-Testing the Estimate

Refutation tests deliberately break the analysis in known ways. If the estimate is valid, breaking it should produce predictable results. If it does not, something is wrong with the model or data.

Placebo Treatment Test — Randomly reassign the treatment label (who received Turing suggestions) while keeping outcomes fixed. Since the"treatment" is now random noise, the estimated ATT should be approximately zero. If it is not, the model is picking up spurious patterns. Implementation: Shuffle the treatment column, re-estimate ATT. Our result: −0.027pp ≈ 0. Verdict: PASS on substance (near zero), but may show"FAIL" on percentage-change tests when the baseline ATT is itself near zero.
Random Common Cause Test — Add a random noise variable (drawn from a standard normal distribution) as an additional covariate and re-estimate the ATT. Since the noise variable is independent of everything, it should not change the ATT meaningfully. A large percentage change (325%) is expected when the baseline ATT is near zero — even a tiny absolute shift (e.g., 0.05 → 0.21pp) produces a huge percentage change. This is a known limitation of percentage-based refutation metrics at the null.
Data Subset Test — Randomly draw 80% of the data and re-estimate the ATT. Repeat multiple times. If the estimate is stable (small range across subsets), the result is not driven by outliers or a few influential observations. Our range of 0.014pp across subsets is very tight — confirming a robust null result.

Subgroup Analysis
By HCP Segment (Exploratory)
Bonferroni correction — When testing multiple hypotheses simultaneously (here, 3 subgroups), the probability of at least one false positive increases. Bonferroni controls this by dividing the significance threshold (α = 0.05) by the number of tests: 0.05 / 3 = 0.0167. A subgroup result is"significant" only if its p-value falls below 0.0167 rather than 0.05. This is the most conservative multiple-testing correction — alternatives like Holm-Bonferroni or Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR) are less strict but require more assumptions. We use Bonferroni because with only 3 tests, the conservatism is minimal, and we label all subgroup analyses as exploratory regardless.
